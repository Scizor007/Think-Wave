What is Nuclear Fission?

Nuclear fission is a reaction in which the nucleus of an atom splits into two or more smaller nuclei, along with the release of a large amount of energy. This process is fundamental to nuclear reactors and atomic bombs.

Working Principle

Nuclear fission occurs when a heavy nucleus, such as uranium-235 or plutonium-239, absorbs a neutron and becomes unstable. The nucleus then splits, releasing energy and additional neutrons, which can further sustain the reaction in a chain process.

Key Components

  • Fuel: Typically uranium-235 or plutonium-239.
  • Neutron Moderator: Slows down neutrons to sustain the chain reaction.
  • Control Rods: Absorb excess neutrons to regulate the reaction.
  • Coolant: Transfers heat from the reactor core.

Applications

  • Used in nuclear power plants for electricity generation.
  • Forms the basis of nuclear weapons.
  • Applications in medical and industrial fields.
