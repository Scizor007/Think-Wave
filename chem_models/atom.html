What is Atomic Structure?

The atomic structure refers to the composition and arrangement of subatomic particles within an atom. It consists of a central nucleus, which contains protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons that orbit in defined energy levels.

Components of an Atom

  • Protons (p⁺): Positively charged particles found in the nucleus.
  • Neutrons (n⁰): Neutral particles located in the nucleus, contributing to atomic mass.
  • Electrons (e⁻): Negatively charged particles that revolve around the nucleus in different energy levels.

Atomic Models Over Time

Scientists have proposed different models to explain the structure of an atom:

  • Dalton’s Model (1803): Atoms were considered indivisible solid spheres.
  • Thomson’s Model (1897): “Plum pudding model” where electrons are embedded in a positively charged sphere.
  • Rutherford’s Model (1911): Discovered the nucleus and stated that electrons revolve around it.
  • Bohr’s Model (1913): Electrons move in fixed orbits or energy levels.
  • Quantum Mechanical Model (1926-Present): Uses probability distributions to describe electron locations.

Energy Levels and Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy different energy levels (shells) around the nucleus. Each shell has a maximum capacity:

  • K Shell (n=1): 2 electrons
  • L Shell (n=2): 8 electrons
  • M Shell (n=3): 18 electrons
  • N Shell (n=4): 32 electrons

Interesting Facts About Atoms

  • Atoms are mostly empty space.
  • The nucleus contains almost all of an atom’s mass.
  • Electrons move at speeds close to that of light.
  • Every element has a unique atomic number, which represents the number of protons.
