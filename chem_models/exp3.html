Overview of the Experiment

J.J. Thomson's Cathode Ray Tube experiment, conducted in 1897, led to the discovery of the electron. This experiment demonstrated that cathode rays are composed of negatively charged particles, later called electrons.

Setup of the Experiment

  • A glass tube was evacuated to remove most of the air.
  • A high voltage was applied between two electrodes inside the tube.
  • A glowing beam (cathode ray) was observed traveling from the cathode (-) to the anode (+).
  • Electric and magnetic fields were used to study the properties of the cathode rays.

Key Observations

  • Cathode rays were deflected by electric and magnetic fields, proving they carried a negative charge.
  • The charge-to-mass ratio (e/m) of these particles was found to be much smaller than that of hydrogen ions, indicating an extremely small particle.
  • This experiment provided direct evidence for the existence of electrons.

Conclusions and Impact

Thomson's findings revolutionized atomic theory, proving that atoms were not indivisible and that subatomic particles existed. This paved the way for modern quantum physics and our understanding of atomic structure.
